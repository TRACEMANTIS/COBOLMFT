# Cut-through vs store-and-forward

A **store-and-forward** switch reads the entire frame into a buffer,
checks the CRC, then forwards it. Latency through the switch is at
least the **serialization delay** for the whole frame (`bits ÷ link
rate`) plus the switch's own constant overhead.

A **cut-through** switch starts forwarding once it has read enough of
the header to know the destination port — typically after 64 bytes.
Latency through the switch becomes a near-constant (a few hundred
nanoseconds for current silicon) regardless of frame size.

The trade-off:

- Cut-through cannot drop a corrupted frame at the switch (it has
  already started forwarding). Errors propagate to the receiving NIC
  to deal with.
- Store-and-forward shines under congestion when buffering helps; on a
  quiet link with large frames the serialization tax dominates.
